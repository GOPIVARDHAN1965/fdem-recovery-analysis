Payment Rate at Closeout — Key Empirical Finding

The assumption that grants require 98% payment completion before closeout is not supported by the historical record. Of 32 closed grants, only 4 (12.5%) reached 98% payment. The distributional evidence points to program-specific thresholds:

ProgramMin Pay%Median Pay%Max Pay%≥98%
PA (n=25)23.7%91.8%100.2%12%
FMAG (n=7)52.7%75.0%83.1%0%

FMAG grants consistently close before reaching 98% payment because fire suppression cost settlements frequently result in de-obligations after initial drawdown. The sequential Q1→Q2→Q3 assumption does not hold for FMAG.

What Determined How Fast Grants Closed

Cross-sectional analysis of the 25 closed PA grants identifies three structural patterns:

Fast closures (under 10 years): DR-3562 (3.1 yrs), DR-3293 (9.1 yrs), DR-1806 (10.7 yrs) — all had small obligation amounts (<$13M), few projects (<35), and zero or minimal LPCs. Payment at closure ranged from 71% to 89%.

Average closures (11–15 years): Most of the 2005–2009 hurricanes fall here. These grants had 100M–750M in obligations and hundreds of LPCs. Obligation was complete before payment reached threshold in all cases.

Long-tail closures (16–19 years): The 2004 hurricane season (Charley, Frances, Ivan, Jeanne) and Wilma (2005). Combined $3.5B obligated. Charley and Frances each had over 1,200 LPCs requiring formal FEMA sign-off. Wilma's 2,387 LPCs produced the longest closeout in the dataset at 18.9 years.

Spearman Correlation: Grant Size vs. Closeout Duration (r = 0.942)

The Spearman rank correlation between log(Total Obligated) and closeout duration for the 25 closed PA grants is 0.942 (p < 0.0001). This is extraordinarily high for program administration data — obligation size alone explains approximately 89% of the variance in PA closeout timing. This single finding motivated the selection of Cox PH over simpler regression models, as it correctly handles the right-censored observations (open grants) that regression ignores.

PA Grant Lifecycle: Current State vs. 7-Year Target
Current median: 11.2 years (observed from 25 closed PA grants). Target: 7-8 years, requiring parallel processing and aggressive LPC/appeals management.
CURRENT STATE — 11.2 Year Median
Phase 1: Declaration & Registration
Months 0–2. Presidential declaration, FEMA-state kickoff, applicant registration. Generally on track.
Phase 2: PW Development
Months 2–24. Damage documentation, scope-of-work development, environmental reviews. Max observed: 6,485 days (17.8 yrs) for one PW to be obligated. Primary Q1 bottleneck.
Phase 3: FEMA Review & Obligation ⚠️ BOTTLENECK
Months 6–48. Each PW reviewed individually. 7,608 active PW versions currently awaiting FEMA determination. On-hold versions have a median wait of 411 days; max 6,260 days. Adds 1–3 years on average.
Phase 4: Work Completion / Period of Performance
Years 1–6+. Subrecipients execute physical repairs. PoP routinely extended. 7,926 projects are 100% work-complete but still open in the administrative system — held by LPC backlog upstream.
Phase 5: Reimbursement Processing
Years 2–12. 65,225 payment transactions across 53 grants. Median processing lag: 39 days. 95th percentile: 451 days. 254 negative payments (recaptures). Payment completion ≠ 98% threshold at closeout.
Phase 6: Large Project Closeout (LPC) ⚠️ PRIMARY BOTTLENECK
Years 5–15. 14,263 LPC records; 12% (1,714) still open. Sequential gate — no grant can close with open LPCs. DR-4337: 329 open. DR-4399: 313 open. Active appeals block LPC resolution: 125 active appeals, 70 awaiting FEMA HQ determination.
Phase 7: Account & Grant Closeout
Years 10–18. 6,928 account closeout records; 678 still open. Sequential dependency on LPC completion. Average 5,721 days in system for all account records. Final reconciliation, audit resolution, closeout letter.
TARGET STATE — 7-Year Goal
Key principle: Run phases in parallel, not sequentially. LPC initiation begins during work completion. Account audit preparation begins 18 months before expected grant close. No phase waits for prior phase to be 100% complete.
Phase 1: Rapid Declaration & Setup — Weeks 0–4
Pre-staged FEMA PA teams. Digital registration. Template-based damage capture. Kickoff within 30 days. Saves: ~1.5 months.
Phases 2+3: Parallel PW Development & Obligation — Months 1–12
Standardized templates + digital submissions. 60-day obligation SLA per PW. Auto-escalation at 90 days. Risk-tiered review depth (small projects expedited). Section 428 Alternative Procedures for eligible large projects eliminates LPC entirely. Saves: ~2.5 years.
Phase 4: Work Completion with Hard PoP — Years 1–3
Maximum 3-year PoP. Quarterly progress gates. LPC initiation begins at 50% work completion, not after. Projects ≥90% complete receive administrative closure. Saves: ~2–3 years.
Phases 5+6: Reimbursements & LPC in Parallel — Years 2–5
30-day reimbursement SLA. LPC runs concurrently with active reimbursements — not after payment is complete. 90-day LPC target for straightforward projects. Dedicated FEMA LPC teams for grants with 50+ open LPCs. Saves: ~5 years on LPC-heavy grants.
Phase 7: Account & Grant Closeout — Years 5–7
Pre-staged audit teams. Account closeout preparation begins 18 months before expected grant close. Digital final reconciliation. Target: grant close by Year 7.

Bottleneck Analysis — Where Time Accumulates
Data-driven identification of the six structural delay factors. Estimates derived from the Cox PH coefficient analysis and cross-sectional comparison of fast vs. slow closures in the 25 closed PA grants.

#1 — LPC Backlog (Primary Bottleneck)

14,263 LPC records across 45 grants. 1,714 (12%) remain open. Each open LPC is a sequential gate — no grant can formally close with any open LPCs. The largest backlogs: DR-4337 (329 open), DR-4399 (313 open), DR-4283 (259 open), DR-4834 (112 open), DR-4177 (112 open). Historical precedent: Wilma (DR-1609) carried 2,387 LPCs and took 18.9 years — the longest in the dataset.

Accounts for ~5–8 additional years on large grants

#2 — Open Appeals Blocking LPC Resolution

2,494 total appeal records. 94.9% are resolved. 125 active appeals remain, with 70 awaiting FEMA HQ determination. Mean dollar amount in controversy: $1.2M. Appeals freeze LPC resolution for the affected project — FEMA will not close an LPC with an active appeal on record. DR-4337 carries 480 appeal records; DR-4486 carries 281. Each FEMA HQ appeal resolution averages 6–18 months.

Accounts for ~2–5 years on high-appeal grants

#3 — Period of Performance Extensions

7,926 projects are 100% work-complete but administratively open. These are held in limbo by LPC or account closeout backlogs upstream. Separately, PoP extension requests are routinely approved without a hard deadline enforcement mechanism. Some Florida 2004-hurricane projects showed active PoP extensions for 10+ years post-declaration.

Adds 2–4 years where hard PoP rules are absent

#4 — PW Obligation Delays

7,608 PW versions currently awaiting FEMA determination (step "1) Awaiting FEMA Determination"). On-hold versions in the active portfolio have a median wait of 411 days; mean 925 days; maximum 6,260 days (17.1 years). Small proportion are on hold (30 records, 0.02%) but the volume awaiting determination is substantial. Each unobligated PW delays Q1 completion and cascades to Q2/Q3.

Adds 1–3 years to Q1 completion on large grants

#5 — Reimbursement Processing Variance

65,225 payable records. Median processing lag: 39 days. 95th percentile lag: 451 days — meaning 5% of all payment requests take over a year to process. 254 negative payment records (recaptures) must be netted against cumulative payments. F Drawdown Date is 100% null, limiting historical payment curve reconstruction. F Warrant Date is 59.4% populated.

Tail variance adds 6–18 months on large grant portfolios

#6 — Grant Scale (Structural Root Cause)

Spearman r = 0.942 between log(Total Obligated) and closeout days. Grant size is not a bottleneck that can be eliminated — it is the underlying structural driver of all other delays. Larger grants have more projects, more LPCs, more appeals, more reimbursement transactions, and more complex account closeout requirements. This is why the Cox PH model's most significant coefficient is on log_total_obligated.

Root structural driver — managed through proportional resourcing
Model Methodology — Why Each Model Was Selected
Three separate models for Q1, Q2, and Q3. Model selection driven by data structure, sample size, censoring mechanism, and predictive performance.

Q1 — Obligation Completion: Rate-Based Extrapolation (Primary)

Training data: ~12 fully-obligated PA grants. 7 FMAG grants.

Why not a parametric model: A Weibull AFT survival model was tested. Concordance = 0.234 — worse than random (0.5) due to age confounding: older grants are disproportionately represented in the completed-obligation set, creating selection bias that no parametric correction resolved with only 12 training events.

  • Rate-based method: if a grant is X% obligated after D days, projected completion = D ÷ X
  • FMAG uses conditional Kaplan-Meier (7 events, tight 391–605 day range — adequate for KM)
  • 95% of SPA obligation activity is administrative — no meaningful Q1 model possible
Model status: Rate-based is serviceable but statistically weak for PA. Confidence intervals are wide. Will improve materially once 20+ PA grants complete obligation.

Q2 — Payment Completion: Payment Velocity (Primary)

Training data: 65,225 payable records across 53 grants. F Warrant Date: 59.4% populated. Submission Date: 100% populated.

Core method: Payment velocity (dollars per day observed from payables transactions) projects remaining days to threshold: remaining_dollars ÷ velocity = days_remaining.

  • Corrected threshold: 92% for PA, 75% for FMAG (empirically derived from 32 closed grants — not assumed)
  • F Drawdown Date is 100% null — cannot use. F Warrant Date used where available; Submission Date as proxy where not
  • Fallback: program-median remaining days for grants with insufficient payment history
  • FMAG: Q2 and Q3 are concurrent — FMAG closes before Q2 threshold is reached
Model status: Velocity method is reliable for grants with 6+ months of payment history. New grants (DR-4834, DR-4828) have high uncertainty due to short observation window.

Q3 — Grant Closeout: Cox Proportional Hazards (Primary for PA)

Training data: 25 closed PA grants (events) + 20 active PA grants (right-censored observations). Total n=45 PA grants.

Why Cox PH over alternatives:

✅ Cox PH — Selected
  • Handles right-censored observations natively (open grants are informative)
  • No distributional assumption on baseline hazard
  • Concordance = 0.957 — validated on held-out grants
  • Produces individual-level survival functions for each active grant
  • Confidence intervals via Breslow estimator
❌ OLS Regression — Rejected

Cannot handle censored observations. Open grants would be coded as zero or excluded — both introduce severe bias. Would underestimate closeout times systematically.
❌ Weibull AFT — Rejected

Requires parametric baseline hazard assumption. Not satisfied by the PA closeout distribution (bimodal, heavy right tail). Also tested: concordance 0.81 — inferior to Cox. Fails on small samples.

Model coefficients (fitted on 25 events / 45 grants):

Log(Total Obligated $)

β = –0.2135

p < 0.0001. Spearman r = 0.942. Dominant predictor. Larger grants have a slightly higher hazard per year open — they attract more dedicated management attention once they mature. But their absolute closeout time is still longer due to structural complexity.

Open Appeals Count

β = –0.0087

p = 0.022. Statistically significant. Each additional open appeal reduces the instantaneous hazard rate (slows toward closeout). High-appeal grants (DR-4337: 480, DR-4486: 281) show the most negative hazard effect.

Grant Age (days)

β = –0.0003

p = 0.030. Significant. Older grants have marginally higher hazard (they have survived longer and are more likely to close soon). This is the time-varying baseline hazard effect that Cox PH captures correctly.

Open LPC Count

β = –0.0043

p = 0.394 — not statistically significant as an independent predictor. LPC count is highly correlated with grant size (r ≈ 0.85), so its marginal effect is absorbed by the log_obligated term. LPCs are mechanistically important — the statistical insignificance reflects collinearity, not irrelevance.

Hybrid Model (v3) -- How Predictions Are Made
Early-stage grants (fewer than 10 LPCs resolved): Cox Proportional Hazards survival model. Trained on 25 closed PA grants. Concordance index = 0.923.

Advanced-stage grants (10+ LPCs resolved): LPC Throughput model. Prediction = (open LPCs / annual LPC resolution rate) + 1 year FEMA overhead. Uses each grant's own historical LPC clearance velocity rather than a survival curve. This directly accounts for current administrative progress and was the key correction to address the Cox PH overestimation for grants like Irma and COVID-19.
Extrapolation Warning: The largest closed PA grant in training data is DR-1609 Wilma ($1.48B, 18.9 yr). Irma ($2.62B), Michael ($2.57B), Ian ($2.97B), and COVID-19 ($4.89B) all exceed the training range. Their predictions require extrapolation and carry substantially higher uncertainty. The model correctly predicts larger grants take longer; exact close years for these four grants should be treated as directional estimates, not precise forecasts.
Model performance: Concordance index = 0.923 (Cox PH). Throughput model used for grants with >10 resolved LPCs. Hybrid approach corrects for Cox PH overestimation in advanced-stage grants (vs. 0.5 random baseline). All 20 active PA predictions fall within the historical PA range of 9.1–18.9 years. DR-4337 (Irma) improved from Cox PH v2 estimate of 2042 to Jun 2029 under the hybrid throughput model, reflecting 355 open LPCs and 0 remaining active appeals.
